Bike computer questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Bike computers are purpose-built devices that give you ride data (speed, distance, navigation) without draining your phone's battery. Choosing one depends on your riding style, budget, and whether you need GPS mapping or just the basics. Below we answer the most common questions to help you pick the right model.

What does a bike computer do that a phone doesn't?

A dedicated bike computer is optimised for life on two wheels. Its screen is readable in direct sunlight, it sips power so you can ride multi-day routes without carrying a battery pack, and it’s usually water-resistant enough to handle a sudden downpour. A phone can do many of the same things, but it’s not designed to stay mounted to your handlebars, exposed to vibrations and weather, for hours at a time. Phones also run down fast with GPS and screen on, and overheating can be an issue on hot summer rides. A bike computer, by contrast, is built to log rides reliably all day. If you ride regularly, especially on longer or more remote roads, a bike computer is a major upgrade in convenience and durability.

How much should I spend on a bike computer?

Prices for bike computers span a wide range, and what you spend should reflect how you ride. At the budget-friendly end, you’ll find basic models that record speed and distance (often using a wheel magnet) and have a simple display. These are great for casual commuters or riders who just want to know how far they’ve gone. Mid-range computers add GPS for accurate tracking, wireless syncing with your phone, and support for sensors like heart rate monitors and cadence. Stepping up to high-end models brings full-colour maps, turn-by-turn navigation with rerouting, and longer battery life measured in many hours. You don’t need to spend a lot to get reliable data, but navigation and mapping are where you’ll see the price climb. Set a budget based on your must-haves, then compare models from well-known brands.

Do I need GPS or will a speed/cadence sensor work?

It depends on the data you care about. A non-GPS computer uses a magnet and sensor on your wheel to measure speed and distance. It’s cheap, runs on a coin battery for months, and is accurate as long as your wheel circumference is set correctly. You won’t get a map or an elevation profile, but you’ll see your pace and distance. A GPS computer tracks your position via satellites, so you can map your route, see altitude changes, and upload your ride for later analysis. You don’t need additional sensors to get speed and distance, but adding a cadence sensor (and a heart rate monitor) makes your training data much richer. Many riders start with GPS and add sensors later. If you want to explore new roads or follow a planned route, go GPS.

What about battery life? How long does a bike computer last?

Battery life varies dramatically by model and features. Basic non-GPS computers that use a wheel sensor can last months or even years on a single coin-cell battery. GPS computers, by contrast, typically run between 10 and 20 hours on a full charge, though some premium models with power-saving modes extend to 30 hours or more. Colour touchscreens and constant map rendering drain the battery faster, while simpler black-and-white displays stretch it. If you plan centuries or multi-day tours, look for a model with at least 20 hours of claimed battery life. Many computers also offer a ‘save battery’ mode that reduces screen refresh and GPS polling. And remember that extreme cold can reduce battery performance, so plan accordingly for winter rides.

Can I get turn-by-turn navigation on a bike computer?

Yes, turn-by-turn navigation is available, but the level of detail varies. High-end models include full colour maps with street names, points of interest, and automatic rerouting if you miss a turn. They can also calculate a route back to your start point with one button press. Mid-range and budget GPS computers typically offer ‘breadcrumb’ navigation: a simple line on a screen showing your intended path against a basic background. You won’t see street names, but you can follow the line. If you primarily ride familiar roads or follow routes you’ve loaded, breadcrumb navigation is perfectly adequate. If you explore unfamiliar terrain frequently, spring for a model with proper maps and rerouting. Also consider screen size--larger screens make map reading much easier at speed.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a bike computer if I have a smartphone?

Not strictly, but a phone makes a poor handlebar computer. Its screen is hard to see in sunlight, GPS drains the battery quickly, and it’s vulnerable to vibration and rain. A dedicated bike computer is more reliable for long rides and frees your phone for calls or emergencies.

Are bike computers waterproof?

Most reputable models have an IPX7 rating, meaning they can survive immersion in up to a metre of water for 30 minutes. Budget computers may only be splash-resistant. Always check the IP rating if you ride in rain often, and avoid models without a stated water-resistance level.

Can I pair my heart rate monitor or power meter?

Virtually all modern GPS bike computers use ANT+ and Bluetooth, so they can connect to heart rate monitors, cadence sensors, speed sensors, and power meters from most major brands. Check sensor compatibility before buying, but the ecosystem is very standardised now.

How do I mount it on my bike?

Every computer comes with a mount. The most common types are a stem mount (which wraps around the handlebar stem) and an out-front mount (which attaches to the handlebar and extends forward). Out-front mounts are popular because they put the screen in your natural line of sight. Many brands sell optional mounts for different setups.

What is GPS breadcrumb navigation?

Breadcrumb navigation shows your planned route as a simple line on a plain background, without detailed maps. It’s common on mid-range and budget computers. It’s effective for following a pre-loaded route, but you won’t see street names or points of interest. For casual touring, it works great.

Do I need a subscription for mapping or data?

Basic functions like recording rides, syncing to apps, and breadcrumb navigation are free on all models. Some brands charge for premium features like live turn-by-turn with voice, weather overlays, or daily training insights. Check each brand’s subscription model before buying if you want those extras.

In shortChoosing the right bike computer comes down to matching features with how and where you ride. Start with your must-haves (GPS, battery life, navigation), set a budget, and read user reviews. A good bike computer will elevate your rides without overwhelming you with data you don’t need.
